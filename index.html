Promoviendo La Esperanza Equitativa/Advancing Equitable Teaching Practices to Reduce Systemic Inequities in Computer Science Courses for Latinx Students

There is a growing emphasis on the importance of improving undergraduate education in STEM disciplines, especially for computer science (CS). Key objectives of this effort include:

  • Updating and expanding CS teaching repertoires by providing comprehensive faculty development for CS instructors to improve their preparedness to academically, socially, and emotionally support Latinx CS students’ academic success.

  • Increasing retention and graduation rates for Latinx undergraduate students pursuing a degree in CS through co-curricular relationship building activities designed to cultivate students’ sense of belonging and well-being in the CS major.

    • Latinx students constitute between 20% and 27% of UNLV’s typical incoming first-year CS cohort (UNLV Decision Support, 2017-2021). The majority of these students, like all UNLV students at every academic level, tend to be first-generation college students which presents additional challenges to success.

    With the increasing Latinx enrollment at UNLV, it is imperative that improvements be made to boost the retention and graduation rates for Latinx undergraduate students pursuing a degree in CS.

    Our proposed project seeks to improve CS teaching and learning by applying Transparency in Learning and Teaching (TILT) techniques that are informed by critical multicultural education (CME) and supported by Open Educational Resources (OER). TILT is a set of teaching techniques that focuses on making transparent to students how and why they are learning and engaging with course content in particular ways (Winkelmes, 2009a; Winkelmes et al., 2016). These techniques have been shown to improve overall student outcomes, but particularly for first generation students in many areas of study, though typically non-STEM. Accordingly, we plan to apply and then measure the impact of these techniques in CS for Latinx students. For example, for early division CS classes, hands-on programming assignments are the primary learning tool for new programmers. Such assignments are typically and arduously, newly generated every semester with little attention to, or improvement in, student assignment completion quality. Improving transparency techniques for such assignments will be a key focus of the proposed project’s TILT-focused efforts in order to concomitantly reduce faculty assignment-generation labor and improve student learning outcomes. Further, by integrating CME and OER into our TILT approach, our goal is to advance equitable and just teaching and learning practices that reduce systemic, especially racial, inequities in higher education.
